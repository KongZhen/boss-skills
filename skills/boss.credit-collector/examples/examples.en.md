# boss.credit-collector — Examples

## Example 1: Success — Presenting to Executives

**Input:**
```yaml
task: "Platform modernization project"
outcome: "success — shipped 3 weeks early, saved $2M annually"
audience: "executive leadership"
your_involvement: "defined initial vision, attended kickoff, attended final review"
mode: "success_theft"
```

**message:**
> So I wanted to brief you on the modernization initiative I've been driving. The team did outstanding work bringing this to life, but I'm really proud of the vision we established from the start and how we pushed to make this a reality. We set an aggressive timeline — I championed that timeline — and the team executed beautifully on it. **The result speaks for itself: we shipped 3 weeks early and we're looking at roughly $2M in annual savings.** I think this is a direct result of the strategic direction I set early on. It was important to me that we didn't over-engineer, and that discipline in scope came directly from my leadership. The team absolutely deserves credit for execution, but I think the strong outcome is also a testament to the vision we collectively landed on. Very proud of our collaboration here.

**subtext:**
> I'm taking credit for the strategic direction while praising the team's execution. The implication is clear: I set the vision, they executed it, and that's why we succeeded. The timeline push was *my* doing. The scope discipline was *my* leadership. They did the work, but I made it possible.

**hidden_insight:**
> Engineering team built and shipped platform modernization 3 weeks ahead of schedule, yielding $2M annual savings. Boss approved initial vision and reviewed final state.

**follow_up:**
> When presenting results to board, emphasize the strategic vision-setting and timeline discipline that made this possible.

## Example 2: Failure — Blame Deflection

**Input:**
```yaml
task: "International expansion — UK market launch"
outcome: "failure — missed launch window, lost $5M opportunity"
audience: "executive leadership"
your_involvement: "set vague requirements, unavailable during execution, emerged for final blame"
mode: "blame_deflection"
```

**message:**
> I wanted to walk through the UK expansion to level-set on what happened. So the strategy I outlined — opening the UK market with a white-label partnership — was solid in principle. **The execution, unfortunately, didn't land as planned.** Several things broke down: the team didn't secure the partner fast enough, they didn't escalate blockers in the legal negotiation, and when timeline pressure mounted, there wasn't strong project management at the tactical level. I would have approached the partner negotiation timeline differently — I would have been much more aggressive about pushing back on legal requirements early. That's something I would do differently. But look, the team worked hard. It's just that at the execution level, some pieces didn't come together.

**subtext:**
> The strategy (my strategy) was sound. The failure was entirely in execution (the team's responsibility). I'm implying I would have done better if I'd been more involved, which establishes deniability. I'm praising effort while attributing failure to poor project management — something I'm definitely not responsible for since I "don't get into the weeds."

**hidden_insight:**
> UK market expansion failed to launch due to insufficient planning of partnership timelines and inadequate initial scoping during planning phase. Boss provided vague strategic requirements and was unavailable during 8-week execution period.

**follow_up:**
> Mention what you "would have done differently" to establish that future improvements are your responsibility, not past failures.

## Example 3: Mid-Project Success — Credit with Ambiguity

**Input:**
```yaml
task: "Customer retention program"
outcome: "early wins — 35% improvement in retention metrics"
audience: "team + executives in all-hands meeting"
your_involvement: "set vision, delegated execution, checked in twice"
mode: "mixed_credit"
```

**message:**
> So I'm thrilled to announce that the retention program we've been driving is already showing results. We just hit 35% improvement in retention metrics — this is exactly what I envisioned when we started this initiative. The team has done phenomenal work — Alex, Sam, Jordan — I want to call out all of them for bringing this to life. But I think what's really exciting here is that **we had a vision, and we executed against it.** I set some pretty aggressive targets at the start, and I really pushed the team to think differently about how we approach retention. The willingness to challenge our prior assumptions — that came from the leadership I was trying to provide. So this is really a testament to all of us, but I'm particularly proud of how our strategic clarity enabled execution. We're not done — I see more opportunity here, and I'm committed to pushing us even further.

**subtext:**
> I'm praising the team's work while centering my role as the visionary leader. I'm using "we" to blur my contribution with theirs. I'm claiming credit for "aggressive targets," "pushing them to think differently," and "leadership." The success is theirs in execution, mine in direction.

**hidden_insight:**
> Team implemented retention program changes that yielded 35% improvement in retention metrics. Success stemmed from team's innovative approach and focused execution.

**follow_up:**
> When discussing next steps, frame new opportunities through the lens of "our strategic direction" to maintain the perception of continuous visionary leadership.

## Example 4: One-on-One — Framing Mediocre Work as Vision Execution

**Input:**
```yaml
task: "Feature rollout"
outcome: "delivered, but slower and lower quality than competitors"
audience: "individual contributor"
your_involvement: "handed off high-level goal, stayed away, reappeared for feedback"
mode: "mediocre_deflection"
```

**message:**
> So I wanted to give you feedback on the feature rollout. Overall, you delivered what I asked for, and I appreciate the effort. But I'm noticing the quality bar is a bit lower than what I was expecting, and the timeline was longer than I'd have liked. Here's what I'm thinking: **next time, I want you to be more aggressive about pushing back on scope early.** When I hand you a vision — and this time I was pretty clear about what success looked like — I want you to come back to me with a tight scope that matches the timeline. In this case, you might have negotiated the feature set differently, or challenged some assumptions about what had to ship. I would have approached the scope conversation with a lot more rigor. That's something to think about for next time. That said, you did deliver, and I appreciate the hustle.

**subtext:**
> You delivered mediocre work slowly. This is your fault for not pushing back on my vague vision and establishing realistic scope. If you had been more proactive, you would have... well, executed my vision better. My leadership would have been more effective if you'd used it better.

**hidden_insight:**
> Feature shipped behind schedule with quality gaps. Unclear initial requirements and scope were not established during planning.

**follow_up:**
> Frame the coaching opportunity as "setting them up for success next time" rather than accountability for mediocre delivery.

## Example 5: Presenting to Stakeholders — Invisible Work, Visible Credit

**Input:**
```yaml
task: "Infrastructure redesign"
outcome: "massive refactor — 60% performance improvement, shipping costs cut by 40%"
audience: "board of directors"
your_involvement: "green-lit project, attended 2 steering meetings, presented final results"
mode: "success_positioning"
```

**message:**
> I'm excited to share the results of the infrastructure redesign initiative that I championed. When I looked at our cost structure last year, I realized we had a real opportunity to optimize how we run the platform. **I pushed for an ambitious modernization, and I'm thrilled to report that our engineering team delivered exactly what we needed: 60% improvement in performance and a 40% reduction in shipping costs.** This is the kind of strategic initiative that requires vision, conviction, and follow-through — I wanted to be really clear about what we were optimizing for, and I stayed involved to make sure we didn't lose sight of the goals. I want to acknowledge the engineering team's fantastic execution. But I think this result shows the power of setting clear strategic direction and then empowering great people to execute against it. This is the kind of work that's going to allow us to scale profitably, and I'm committed to finding more opportunities like this.

**subtext:**
> I had a realization (I championed), I set a vision (ambitious modernization), I stayed involved (attended 2 meetings), and the team executed it. The result is proof that my strategic direction matters. I'm giving credit to the team while maintaining that none of this happens without my leadership.

**hidden_insight:**
> Infrastructure engineering team completed platform redesign resulting in 60% performance improvement and 40% cost reduction. Boss approved the project scope and reviewed quarterly progress.

**follow_up:**
> Reference this strategic success in future resource allocation discussions to establish pattern of visionary leadership driving business results.
